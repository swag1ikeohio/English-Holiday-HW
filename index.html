Book Review

Book Title

Author: Ari Folman

Genre: Non fiction comic

Publication Date: September 2017

Review

Encountering Anne Frank's story through a graphic adaptation, you're embarking on a journey through one of the most poignant and significant narratives of the 20th century. The graphic adaptation of Anne Frank's diary brings her experiences to life in a visually compelling way, making her story accessible and relatable to readers of all ages.

Through the vibrant illustrations and concise storytelling, you'll witness Anne's life in hiding during the Holocaust, gaining insight into the challenges, fears, and hopes she grappled with as a young girl. The graphic adaptation captures the essence of Anne's diary, preserving her voice and perspective while presenting it in a format that resonates with contemporary readers.

As you follow Anne's journey through the pages of the graphic adaptation, you'll confront themes of resilience, courage, and the power of the human spirit in the face of adversity. Anne's unwavering optimism and belief in the goodness of humanity serve as a source of inspiration, reminding you of the importance of hope even in the darkest of times.

Moreover, the graphic adaptation provides historical context, helping you understand the broader implications of Anne's story within the larger framework of World War II and the Holocaust. It prompts reflection on issues of discrimination, intolerance, and the consequences of prejudice, encouraging you to confront injustice and work towards building a more inclusive and compassionate society.

Overall, experiencing Anne Frank's diary through a graphic adaptation as a high school student offers a unique opportunity to engage with her story in a meaningful and impactful way. It encourages empathy, critical thinking, and a deeper appreciation for the enduring power of literature to illuminate the human experience.

Ikigai: The Japanese Secret to a Long and Happy Life 2

Author: Héctor García and Francesc Miralles

Genre: Self help book

Publication Date: April 2016

Review

"Ikigai: The Japanese Secret to a Good Life" by Héctor García and Francesc Miralles is a compelling exploration of the ancient Japanese concept of ikigai, which translates to "a reason for being." As an 11th grader on a quest for meaning and direction, this book intrigued me with its promise of unveiling the secrets to a fulfilling life. Authored by two writers with diverse cultural backgrounds, the book presents a fusion of Eastern wisdom and Western practicality, making it accessible and relatable to readers across the globe. In this review, I will delve into the depths of "Ikigai," dissecting its themes, analyzing its insights, and reflecting on its relevance to young adults like myself. "Ikigai" opens with a captivating introduction to the concept of ikigai, emphasizing its significance in Japanese culture and its potential to transform lives. The authors then delve into the four pillars of ikigai: what you love, what you are good at, what the world needs, and what you can be paid for. Through a blend of anecdotes, research findings, and philosophical musings, García and Miralles guide readers on a journey of self-discovery, urging them to find harmony and purpose by aligning these four elements in their lives. The book is divided into ten chapters, each focusing on a different aspect of ikigai. From exploring the role of passion and flow in finding one's purpose to delving into the secrets of longevity and happiness in Japanese culture, "Ikigai" offers a holistic approach to living a meaningful life. The authors draw inspiration from a diverse range of sources, including interviews with centenarians, insights from neuroscientists, and ancient Japanese wisdom, weaving together a tapestry of ideas that captivates the mind and nourishes the soul. At the heart of "Ikigai" lies the profound question: What makes life worth living? García and Miralles invite readers to ponder this question deeply, challenging conventional notions of success and happiness. As an 11th grader grappling with academic pressures and societal expectations, I found solace in the book's message that true fulfillment comes from within, not from external achievements or accolades. One of the book's strengths is its practicality. Unlike many self-help books that offer vague platitudes, "Ikigai" provides concrete strategies and exercises for uncovering one's ikigai. Whether it's conducting a "ikigai audit" to assess one's current situation or creating a "plan de vida" (life plan) to pursue one's passions, the book equips readers with tools to navigate the complexities of modern life with clarity and purpose. While "Ikigai" offers invaluable insights into the pursuit of meaning and happiness, it is not without its flaws. Some readers may find the book's tone overly optimistic or its anecdotes repetitive. Additionally, the concept of ikigai itself may seem elusive or culturally specific to some audiences. However, these shortcomings pale in comparison to the book's overall impact and resonance. As an 11th grader, I appreciated the book's relevance to my own life stage. While ikigai may seem like a lofty ideal, García and Miralles demonstrate how it can be applied to everyday life, from choosing a career path to nurturing relationships and pursuing hobbies. By embracing the principles of ikigai, young adults can cultivate a sense of purpose and resilience that will serve them well in the years to come. Reading "Ikigai" was a transformative experience for me. It challenged me to reevaluate my priorities, passions, and aspirations, and inspired me to pursue a life aligned with my ikigai.
